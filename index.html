A European and multi-disciplinary research network

Initiated in 2009 from a group of dedicated young postdocs, SnowNet developed a unique set of skills  across atmosphere physics, chemistry and biology to understand the impact of human activities and monitor climate change in the Arctic.

Svalbard, a unique site to study climate change

The glacier Kongsvegen in September

A sensitive and changing environment at reach 

Due to its unique location far North at the end of the Gulf Stream, Svalbard is a place of choice to study snow and ice in the context of climate change. Because snow covers large parts of the land and sea in winter and melts, subtle changes in air temperature can lead to large changes and affect feedbacks on climate, glacier, animals and plants. However, basic measurements of land climate variables such as temperature, snow thickness, wind speed, etc. are currently limited in the Arctic, including Svalbard.

The Hub: Ny Ålesund Research Station

Snow is a key parameter of the Arctic water and energy cycle, and is an important variable of the climate system of Svalbard, thereby requiring sustainable monitoring of the physical drivers over large regions. Currently, a new network of high-tech low-cost sensors collect SIOS’ snow core data on Kongsvegen and are yet to broadcast data to the scientific and local communities. It is necessary to ensure that this "live" system installed near Ny Ålesund works as soon as possible prior to the likely surge of Kongsvegen glacier.
