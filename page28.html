Glacier calving in Southern Greenland

by Adrien Wehrlé and Maurice van Tiggelen, glaciologists at XXX, XX XXX 2020

Image Description

South-Greenland, August 2020

After a few weeks of uncertain travel restrictions and change in plans, it’s hard to believe it but we're finally here in Narsarsuaq, South Greenland. Deep in the Tunulliarfik Fjord, and along the floodplains of Kiattuut glacier, lies this small settlement that will be our base for the next two weeks.

Better understand a large melt anomaly of the ice-sheet

Not so far from here, 75 km East on the ice sheet, lies the so-called "Q-transect". It's a transect of weather stations and mass balance observations operated by the Geological Survey of Denmark and Greenland (GEUS) as part of the Programme for Monitoring of the Greenland Ice Sheet (PROMICE) . What makes this place so special is the huge ablation rates that have been measured along the years. In 2012, the lowest site, called 'QAS_L', recorded 8.5 meters of ice ablation within one single summer season. Even more striking is that almost 1 meter of melt was recorded in just a few days in July 2012. To better understand the anomalous melt in this region is why GEUS and Utrecht University started a cooperation at QAS-L in 2019. These automatic stations operate year round, endure the tough Greenland weather conditions and therefore require maintenance once a year.
This time, it's us!

Johannes taking the “Findelengletscher glacier fore-field challenge”: Finding a path through this some-times unstable area can be difficult. It’s made up of loose rocks, little streams, dead ice and mud; the glacier itself was right here only a few decades ago. (photo credit: Christophe Ogier/ETH Zurich)

First day of fieldwork. Blue sky, not even a lonely cloud. Today we have to pick up the shipment in Narsaq, and install the new instruments on the ice sheet. After a beautiful boat ride and some negotiations in the harbor container hall, here we are waiting for the helicopter to pick us up in Narsaq. Distracted by all the icebergs floating around, Maurice cuts his fingers while preparing his lunch sandwich. Fortunately, Adrien is well prepared and applies first aid without a blink.
Jason, professor at GEUS and supervisor of the fieldwork, calls on the sat phone: "guys, ETA of the helicopter is 1am". We're ready. The excitement grows as the distant engine noise gets louder and louder. Within a few seconds, there it comes starting his landing. We squeeze all the instruments and tools in the helicopter. Greenland ice sheet, here we come!

The views from the helicopter are incredible. Icebergs, calving fronts, crevasses and even a French sailing ship from the Unu Mondo expedition. Hardly a coincidence to find it here as its crew will be joining us today to see what kind of work we are doing on the stations. . We arrive at QAS-L. The first feeling is “wow, the stations are still standing !”. The second feeling is “now let’s get the work done!”. We don’t have a lot of time, yet we have a lot to do. Today we install the new open path gas analyzer, change the batteries, connect three new large solar panels and make sure everything is running as it should. After a couple of hours of intensive work , we know that the pilot approaching us is the sign that time’s up, we have to go. We wrap up our work, Adrien attaches the last cables while Maurice checks the status of the station and exclaims: “It’s running !”. What a relief! We quickly pack our gear and leave. Within a few tens of minutes we are back on land, preparing out night camping next to the ice sheet.This was just the first day of 2 weeks in Greenland for some, or 4 weeks for others. Needless to say that the locally brewed QajaQ beer tasted particularly good that evening !

Helicopter to the station transect

The next few days, we sailed back to Narsarsuaq with the rest of the team, and helped locals to plant trees, together with the organization Greenland Trees. Hard work, camping, muskox and, icing on the cake, the mighty northern lights. 

But we are not done on the Q-transect yet. After a few days of planting trees at the Qanassiassat plantation, we prepare for our next trip on the ice sheet. The weather is uncertain. Low clouds, rain, hard wind, probably fresh snow on the ice. Yet, we decided to give our trip a go. Allan, engineer at GEUS, comes to pick us up and we head for the highest station: ‘QAS-U’.

We see the station in the distance. We also see the deep crevasses, lying just a few tens of meters from the station. And remnants of a snow storm. We jump out of the helicopter. The ice is slippery, and it’s raining now. Yes, it’s raining. We are standing 1000m on the ice sheet and it’s raining. Allan gives the instructions, and we start disassembling the old instruments and redrilling new ones. Water keeps filling the drill holes, and then the drill gets stuck. We pull it out manually, then we install the new instruments. A new wind power turbine for the Snowfox cosmic ray sensor, hygroclips on the weather stations, snow height rangers... There is a whole list. A few hours later, we’re done. And there’s even time to check on QAS-L station on the way back, yey!

The scenery from QAS-U to QAS-L is stunning. The snowed-in crevasses higher-up slowly make space to the more and more abundant melt streams. The ice gets darker and darker. Ice hummocks get larger and larger. We fly over site ‘QAS-M’, where we camped during last year’s visit. This year, Allan will go there together with Jason and Dirk, from Greenland Guidance. We land at QAS-L. Yes, the stations are still there, and yes, they are still working! Why wouldn't they be, right ? We drill a new stake, finish connecting the solar panels on the turbulence station, get the first data and take a lot of pictures. And then it’s time to go back to Narsarsuaq. How quickly time flies when working in the field !

Sitting here now, confined back home and writing this story, makes us realize even more how special this was. You realize how fast the weather conditions can change in South Greenland, how different the surface can look like. You realize how friendly the locals are, how good a beer tastes after a day of manual work. But most importantly, you realize there is always a story behind a data file with field measurements.

Adrien Wehrlé
PhD candidate

Johannes Landmann studied geography and meteorology at the Universities of Bonn, Germany, and Innsbruck, Austria. He is currently pursuing a doctoral degree in glaciology at ETHZ, focusing on near real-time glacier ensemble modelling and data assimilation techniques.

Maurice van Tiggelen
PhD candidate

Maurice van Tiggelen is a PhD candidate at the Institute for Marine and Atmospheric research Utrecht (IMAU, Utrecht University, the Netherlands). Understanding the drivers of extreme melt rates on the Greenland ice sheet is the main motivation of his research.
