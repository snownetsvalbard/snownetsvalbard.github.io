Our purpose

New technologies available thanks to recent development in open software and hardware technology allow us to build an internet of things (IoT) network in remote areas of Svalbard to collect a wide range of snow, glacier and climate data. Such instruments are now capable of long range transmission with low power consumption for small data bandwidth (~kB per day). Bringing such technology into Arctic conditions (long winter night, cold temperatures) is a challenge but a needed step to decrease cost and environmental footprint in instrument deployment and maintenance. Particular efforts in energy consumption and harvesting, communication technology, and handling of data must be considered to sustain the long dark and cold winter season.

The problematic

In Ny-Ålesund, Kongsvegen is a medium size glacier, and a surge type glacier, with the last surge known being estimated in 1948. That glacier is probably the most equipped in terms of geophysical instruments over the entire archipelago, and is also a route used by the local community to commute to Longyearbyen. Since 2016, we observed an increase in its velocity. In September 2020, the glacier velocity peaked at 39 m per year, 50% more than last year, the signs of a forthcoming surge. We do not know how long it will remain safe to drive with snowmobiles on the glacier as crevasses open and extend during the surge. Combining these two information above, it is urgent to install a functional network in order to collect data with minimal access to the field, minimizing visits and therefore reducing potential needs for helicopter access in the near future.
