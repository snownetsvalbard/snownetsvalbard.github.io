Snow crystal properties

Snow as an archive of our past

Snow is at the cross-roads of liquid water, hard ice and volatile vapour. Snow depends on each of this state's contrasting properties. For instance, snow is a great insulator due to pockets of air trapped between the snow crystals. This internal space also lets water vapour conveys heat from the ground to the surface and at the same time reshapes snow crystals along the way. However, the formation of ice lenses during warm days or rain acts as a lid trapping warmer air below in winter and in spring blocking the downward flow of meltwater in the snow.

Which impurities do we find in the Arctic

When studying snow, we pay very much attention to its properties such as porosity, conductivity, water vapour content and density that varies during the winter and spring season. These are very abstract concepts compare to what we can measure in the field (density, temperature, grain size, hardness) but are necessary to understand the physics behind metamorphosis. Let's see how changes in these properties affect the formation of depth hoar.

The formation of depth hoar
Depth hoar is a type of snow that forms inside the snow pack during very cold winter days and a thick enough insulating snow pack.

Your snow questions have not all been answered yet? Here are a few more:

What does snow need to form in the atmosphere?

Snow needs a nuclei, which can be particles light enough to be in the air such as dust/aerosols or even sometimes viruses. 

Why can snow fall even at temperatures above 0 degree celsius?

Snow can fall at a wide range of temperatures from -2 to 2 degree celsius. The reason for having snow even at positive temperatures is colder air from up in the atmosphere or the water content in the air. (blablalbla)

How many branches have a snow crystal?

A snow crystal always have six branches or three axes of symmetry but can have a wide range of shape. See here pictures from American colleagues at the NSIDC.
