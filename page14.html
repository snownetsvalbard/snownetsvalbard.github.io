A streaming portal

Near-real time data streaming from automated instruments in remote places is now at reach. Rapid development in open source software and hardware technology to build a global “Internet of Things” (IoT), in which the status and data from any “thing” may be accessed wirelessly through the internet, has reduced costs to develop, deploy and maintain instruments capable of long-range data transmission with low power demands. Networks of low-cost sensors can now be spread over large regions to capture greater spatial and temporal variability of Earth system processes. This new approach is key in resolving scaling issues between remote sensing, climate modeling, and in-situ observations. Svalbard is uniquely situated to take advantage of such technology, thanks to its broad and expanding cellular coverage. Existing monitoring stations can now be equipped with new wireless sensor networks for minimal cost.

SnowLive is a bridge between researchers and the local population and a push for Open Data in Svalbard

Kongsvegen

Kongsvegen area close to Ny-Ålesund is the most equipped glacier in terms of instrumentation in Svalbard. It last surged in 1948, and has been slowly building toward a new surge since then. In addition, Kongsvegen is the main winter transportation corridor to Ny- Ålesund. If surging, the glacier surface will become heavily crevassed and hazardous to travel, making it difficult to maintain observation sites and preventing land access to Ny-Ålesund for many years. In 2020, the glacier velocity increased by 50% compared to 2019 (J. Kohler, pers. comm.). It is therefore urgent to deploy the IoT network now and develop a workflow routine for quality control, meta-data check, formatting for database storage and release to SIOS and the Svalbard communities (Fig. 1).
